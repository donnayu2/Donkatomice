Cell Filtration and Extraction As A Method To Detect and Treat Pancreatic Cancer

The presence of circulating tumor cells (CTCs), which are metastasized cancer cells that can be found in the bloodstream, is the key to the effective detection and treatment of pancreatic cancer. In our study, we plan to use a novel filtration method involving Epithelial Cell Adhesion Molecules (EpCAMs) and blood mixing cell technology to change the way pancreatic cancer is detected and to personalize its treatment.

CTCs and Metastasis

Circulating Tumor Cells(CTCs) are cancerous cells that break off from the original tumor and enter the bloodstream through the process known as metastasis. They are identical in structure to the host cancer and are difficult to locate due to their relative scarcity.

Current Technology

The CellSearch Assay is currently the only FDA-approved CTC enumeration processs. It involves adding EpCAM molecules to blood from the patient, which seek out the EpCAM antigen expressed by the CTCs. After this it uses a number of filtration processes in order to remove other types of cells from the sample. Present technology is not precise enough to collect a sufficient sample of CTCs and also requires the manipulation of their structure, which makes them only useful for enumeration.

Future Applications

The ability to effectively extract pancreatic CTCs will aid in the development of a culture media that encourages the growth of the cells. The "liquid biopsy" procedure we propose can be applied to the population on a large scale. Patients will be tested for pancreatic cancer every year with a blood test. Additionally, this will provide a less invasive, more specialized treatment plan for patients.
